    Why? The Rise of Specialty Coffee

    With the rapid increase in popularity from Starbucks and other coffee chains, overall specialty coffee consumption has risen over the last two decades.

    • Occasional specialty coffee consumption up 5% within the last two decades
    • Weekly specialty coffee consumption 13% within the last two decades
    • Daily specialty coffee consumption 30% within the last two decades

    The motivation for this project can be explained with this graph showing that for the last 20 years, there has been a sharp increase in the rise of daily specialty coffee consumption. With the help of our dashboard, we hope to provide easy to digest visualizations and tips for coffee connoisseurs all around.

    Where is coffee grown?

    The Coffee Belt

    Most of the world's coffee is grown in the coffee belt, a defined area between the Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn, 25 degrees north and 30 degrees south of the equator and passes through some 70 countries.

    The region's tropical climates supply a rich environment perfect for growing coffee.

    Measuring Production

    Coffee beans are usually transported in large jute 60-kg (130 pound) sacks. Originating in Brazil, this type of bag became a worldwide standard and unit of measurement.

    Statistics on coffee production are often expressed in 60-kg bags.

    How coffee is evaluated

    Evaluation

    Farms can get their coffee to get evaluated by certified Q-Graders in order to receive specialty coffee status.

    • The grading process is done at every step after processing
    • Evaluators have to become certified in either robusta or arabica beans.
    • Evaluators have to undergo a series of tests and certifications

    Each stage of the process is done using rigorous testing methodology set by the Specialty Coffee Association.

    Detailed descriptions of Attributes

    Farms can get their coffee to get evaluated by certified Q-Graders in order to receive specialty coffee status.

    Q-Score

    The Q-Graders evaluate the coffees through various attributes. All the attributes are summed up and scored out of 100.

    • Graders first visually inspect the beans to ensure no defects
    • The beans are roasted, then evaluated through cupping
    • Attributes are given a score from 6 to 10

    Specialty Coffee Status

    Coffees that score at least 60 is considered commercial grade coffee, while coffees that score above 80 are considered specialty grade.

    4. Insight: All Q-grades are not created equal

    Does acidity have the same impact on total points as sweetness? Does that association hold across different regions? The viz above shows us that each quality not only seems to be drawn from a different scoring distribution, but also seems to mean different things for different regions of production.

    Understanding these associations are vital in understanding what each score really means. Be sure to remember the following when evaluating q-grades:

    • Grades do not denote direction. An acidity grade of 10 does not infer an acidic coffee, but rather that the coffee's respective acidity works for its profile.
    • Clean cup, sweetness, and uniformity are all ranked as error rates out of 10 tested cups, so they rank much higher on average, but typically have heavier impacts on total points.
    • Critics typically evaluate against the common consensus for what a given coffee should be. Understanding these regional paradigms will give greater insight into what each score actually means.

    This information should enable you to decompose any Q-grade, but take it with a grain of salt. At the end of the day, any score is subjective. Your tastes may vary, and that's okay.
