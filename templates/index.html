    Did You Know?

    In the past decade terrestrial ecosystems have absorbed approximately 30% of CO2 emissions caused by human activities slowing down the increase in atmospheric CO2 and mitigating climate change.

    Deforestation affects us all

    Trees act as carbon sinks, absorbing CO2. When forests are cleared, they release CO2 and other greenhouse gases, causing around 10% of global warming. Preserving forests is crucial to combating climate change. [*]

    Carbon Cycle and Carbon Sinks

    Carbon is a fundamental element that serves as the building block of all life on Earth, essential for the formation of proteins and DNA. Additionally, it plays a crucial role in regulating the planet's temperature and is a critical component of the food chain. Moreover, carbon serves as a major source of energy for our global community.

    It is in a constant state of exchange, both within and between the Earthâ€™s four major carbon reservoirs: atmosphere, oceans, land, and fossil fuels. This intricate carbon cycle mirrors the flow of energy throughout the planet, making it an essential process for maintaining the balance of carbon in our ecosystem.

    Carbon dioxide (CO2) found in the atmosphere can be absorbed by natural carbon sinks, such as oceans, forests, and soil. Alternatively, carbon capture and storage (CCS) and direct air capture (DAC) technologies offer artificial solutions for capturing and storing CO2 emissions from industrial processes or directly from the air. These systems help prevent the release of greenhouse gasses into the atmosphere and reduce carbon emissions. These natural and artificial carbon sinks can help mitigate the impact of climate change on our planet.

    Why should we study GPP and NEE?

    GPP and NEE

    Gross Primary Productivity (GPP) is the total amount of carbon dioxide that is absorbed by plants during photosynthesis in a given period of time. This process involves the conversion of carbon dioxide and water into organic matter and used by plants for growth and energy.

    At least half of this production is respired by the plants and released back into the atmosphere. The difference between the amount of CO2 absorbed through photosynthesis and the amount released into the atmosphere is the Net Ecosystem Exchange (NEE).

    Insight to the global carbon cycle and effects of CO2 on environment

    Understanding the global carbon cycle and how ecosystems react to changing climates is pivotal to mitigating the harmful impact of human activities such as deforestation and fossil fuel usage. In this context, studying Gross Primary Productivity (GPP) and Net Ecosystem Exchange (NEE) plays a critical role.

    Not only does it offer valuable insight into the global carbon cycle, but it also helps us understand the effects of human activities on the environment. Armed with this knowledge, we can develop effective policies and strategies that are crucial for combating climate change and safeguarding the future of our planet.
