Yearly Trends

Looking at the area around Bolivia, Modis model estimates a large increase in GPP from 2001-2020 while the CEDAR ML Model shoes very minimal increase.

Discrepancies such as these are crucial in understanding the work that still needs to be done in this area. The CEDAR models differ from MODIS as they created models using direct CO2 fertilization effects. This additional model input clearly estimates GPP differently. With these models just being estimates, this figure should help further the call to action to continue to build flux towers and obtain more accurate GPP metrics to improve models such as these.

Modis vs CEDAR ML

Using CarbonTrack’s model comparison feature, we are able to highlight areas on the map where two models estimate GPP very differently. With the limited amount of flux towers in the region, it is impossible to know the true GPP values and thus determine which modeling technique predicts closest to the true value. This helps shine a light on a big issue in the field - with limited amount of flux towers in the world, GPP modeling and upscaling has a very low accuracy ceiling. With more flux towers around more diverse regions, the models could be much more performant, providing researchers with accurate GPP estimates as we continue our fight in climate change.

Model Comparison Time-series Analysis

The figure to the right shows time-series analysis of South America, comparing four models using the Multi Map functionality. While the three CEDAR model variations show a similar downward trend, the MODIS model shows almost no change in GPP over the 2015 to 2020 time range.

A Tale of Two Decades

CarbonTrack’s Yearly Trend feature helps policy makers see GPP trends in their region overtime. This figure is showing the differences between 2001-2010 and 2011-2020 which highlights huge difference we can see in GPP change in just two decades.

Yearly Trends

The Model Comparison feature again provides a look into how the MODIS’ satellite imagery based model differs from CEDAR’s model with direct CO2 fertilization effects. The image here shows the drastic estimation differences around large river sources, the Nile and Amazon rivers.

Call to Action

These examples are just a glimpse at some of the insights which can be gained through the use of CarbonTrack. While these insights are certainly something to celebrate, we believe that the work is just beginning. The discrepancies between the models is something to take note of and should motivate more researchers to continue to build more sophisticated GPP models. Additionally, these insights should motivate climate researchers and policy makers to build more flux towers across the globe to obtain more accurate GPP measurements so that models can be trained on rich and contextual GPP data.
